Culture

Originally created as a basketball shoe, the Converse All-Star was the first shoe to ever be endorsed by a celebrity athlete—in this case, the 1920s basketball player Charles ‘Chuck’ Taylor.

Though originally an elite sports shoe—the official shoe of the Olympic games from 1936 to 1968, the official athletic training shoe of young American soldiers during World War 2, and worn by ninety percent of high school, college and professional basketball players by the 1960s—they lost traction to newer technologies in the 1970s, eventually regaining popularity in the 1980s as casual,retro-style footwear.

By the 90s, Chucks had become the shoe of choice among subcultures, artists and musicians, symbolizing a timeless can-do attitude and epitomizing mercurial reinvention on one’s own terms.

In January 2013, Converse announced plans for a new headquarters building. It was constructed near North Station in downtown Boston, on the Lovejoy Wharf, overlooking the Charles River as part of a major site overhaul and restoration of public waterfront access.The 10-story 214,000-square-foot (19,900 m2) office building includes a permanent music recording studio for the new "Converse Rubber Tracks" project, 5,000-square-foot (460 m2) gym with a separate yoga studio designed in partnership with Nike, and a new 3,500-square-foot (330 m2) retail Flagship Store

As of 2019, Converse sold products through 109 company-owned retail stores in the United States and 63 stores in international markets.
